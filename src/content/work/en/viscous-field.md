      *Viscous Field* focuses on the experience of photographing the Xueiyuan
      area, treating it as a perceptual condition that exists between movement
      and stillness. The images are not intended as landscape or documentation,
      but are instead reorganized and generated within the site itself.

      The humid air, the penetration of light, and incidental structural traces give the space an unstable continuity—neither fully paused nor truly in motion. The images therefore become closer to an extended act of seeing, rather than a representation of place.

      Within this condition, Xueiyuan is not understood as a fixed location, but as a “Viscous Field” that can be repeatedly entered. Images are stretched, reconfigured, and continuously produced into shifting modes of reading.

      ### Known Unknowns


      #### *Known unknowns refer to things that we know exist, yet have not fully understood.*


      *Known Unknowns* emerged from a year's worth of repeatedly photographing the same place.


      Through each return, I accumulated a growing archive of photographs and instant prints. Rather than being arranged chronologically or structured into a single narrative, they are presented through accumulation and juxtaposition, forming an ever-evolving visual archive.


      As individual photographs begin to lose their role as standalone subjects, the act of viewing shifts toward navigating, comparing, and connecting images. These fragments gradually construct an understanding of the place while simultaneously revealing new questions that remain unanswered.


      There is no fixed reading order, nor a single way of viewing the work. Instead, viewers are invited to take on the role of an investigator, forming their own interpretations through the connections between the images.

      ### Constant


      #### *Constant: A quantity whose value remains unchanged throughout a given process.*


      The title Constant originates from a term deeply rooted in science and engineering.


      While repeatedly returning to the same places and organizing photographs taken over the course of a year, I began to wonder: if life also has its own constants, what would they be?


      I realized that what truly remained were never specific landscapes, but recurring ways of seeing and traces of everyday life—a familiar afternoon light, the habit of looking down at the ground, the roads I unconsciously walked again and again, and ordinary moments so subtle that they would otherwise go unnoticed. These were never truly permanent; rather, through repeated visits and quiet returns, they gradually became stable reference points within my life.


      *Constant* reinterprets this scientific concept as an observation of time. What begins as the ordinary gradually becomes the everyday through repetition; what becomes familiar is often mistaken for the permanent; and only when confronted with impermanence do we begin to realize that the things accompanying us through a period of our lives have quietly become our own constants.


      In mathematics, a constant is defined as a value that never changes. In life, however, a constant is not something that remains forever unchanged, but something that repeatedly appears throughout a chapter of our lives—even if it is ultimately destined to disappear.

      ### Render


      "Fujifilm isn't made for live performance photography."


      *Render* began with a statement often heard in discussions about concert photography.


      In live performance photography, speed, autofocus accuracy, and reliable subject tracking are often considered essential. As a result, Fujifilm is rarely regarded as the first choice for this genre.

      This led me to ask: Does live performance photography have to pursue a faithful reproduction of the stage?


      *Not a reproduction, but a translation.*


      Rather than documenting an entire performance, Render preserves the fragments that remain in memory. Light, gesture, expression, and emotion are no longer just fleeting moments on stage; they become images that are reinterpreted and reorganized through the act of remembering.


      *To remember is to render.*


      Photography is not about preserving every moment of a performance, but about reconstructing the images that continue to stay with us.
